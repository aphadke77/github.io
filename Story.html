Challenges & Solutions

Handover Challenges for Capital Projects

  • Plant Breakdown Structure alignment with standard
  • Mapping FLOC to Tags
  • STEP files in 3D model - 3D model requirements or standard
  • Vendor data - manufacturer, model serial no
  • Equipment data - start-up date, warranty information
  • EDW sync with asset specific data sources EDD, FSR etc.
  • Tag / Equipment class alignment with Engineering Information Specifications
  • Tagging standard alignment and any outliers
  • Doc to Tag relations should be available based on EIS / Operations and Project Information requirement

Process Scenario 1

SAP doesn't create new FLOCs unless the associated tags appear on company drawings. This restriction creates a challenge when aligning data from multiple sources.

Process Scenario 2

The legacy asset datasheets are in PDF format. Whenever an update needs to be made, these PDFs are redlined. Repeated updates to these datasheets have made it nearly impossible to correctly identify the latest revisions or track back to older versions.

People Scenario 1

Employee turnover, contractor retentions leading to knowledge retention issues.

Real-World Stories

Overcoming Challenging Situations

Situation: We faced a significant challenge when a critical project deliverable was delayed. This delay coincided with the scheduled leave of the engineer responsible for processing the deliverable, which created an unexpected situation.

Action: Anticipating potential delays as a risk, we had proactively considered several strategies. First, we cross-trained other engineers to ensure they could step in when needed. Second, we identified a flexible resource who could handle overflow work. Additionally, we began automating parts of the process to minimize reliance on specific individuals and improve long-term resilience.

Result: Because of our foresight and preparation, we were able to manage the delay without impacting the overall project timeline. The cross-trained engineers and the flexible resource effectively handled the workload, and the automation efforts paid off by reducing dependency on specialized skills.

Influencing Others

Situation: During the pandemic, our workplace experienced a sudden shift to remote work. Once the company decided we should return to the office for two days a week, it caused disruption since my team had settled into the work-from-home model.

Task: I needed to influence my team to embrace this new change and transition back to a hybrid working model smoothly.

Action: To lead by example, I gradually started going to the office in person and shared my positive experiences with the team. I arranged informal meetups at cafes for brainstorming sessions and casual connections. These efforts created a sense of need to meet in person among the team members.

Resolution: This gradual, experience-based change process helped the team adapt seamlessly to the new hybrid working model, which has now become our new normal.

Converting Challenge into Opportunity

Situation: A client faced a significant challenge with their proprietary design tool used for reporting engineering data. The tool had reached a dead end as no one possessed the skills to maintain or enhance it.

Task: My objective was to turn this challenge into an opportunity and deliver results that met the client's expectations.

Action: My team and I brainstormed and deliberated on the issue extensively. We came up with an innovative solution by redefining the problem. We proposed using a different technology and approach, which the client embraced. This new solution was not only sustainable but also scalable for future needs. We successfully redesigned the tool and as a result, were awarded a long-term Annual Maintenance Contract (AMC) for the tool and its future developments.

Resolution: We utilized the design tool's API to interface with a new application built on the .NET framework.

Areas for Improvement

Example 1: One of my weaknesses is occasionally becoming overly focused on perfecting details, which can slow down project timelines. To address this, I'm actively working on balancing attention to detail with overall project progress. I've implemented time management techniques like the Pomodoro method and timeboxing. These methods help me allocate specific time slots for detailed work and set limits on how long I spend on tasks. By doing so, I maintain a broader perspective on project goals and deadlines while still ensuring quality work.

Example 2: I need to do a better job of delegating my work. Being technical comes with an innate desire to analyze and perform many activities myself. I need to strike the right balance between not getting bogged down in the details and keeping my hands-on skills fresh.
